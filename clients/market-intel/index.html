November 2013
All Generations, Apply Within

Tweet

“A penny saved is a penny earned.”
“You can’t take it with you.”
“Hard work is its own reward.”
“Money doesn’t grow on trees.”
“Work smarter, not harder.”

I’m sure we each have our own memories of the ‘advice’ we received while growing up. Early on it seemed there was never a shortage — and by the time I was a pre-teen I could almost anticipate when one of these homilies was about to stall me in my tracks. As is natural, with age these teachings were filtered through personal experiences, and with time, became less gospel, and more guidance.

These proverbial sayings were meant to be endearing, moral and ethical lessons. The messengers — our parents, grandparents, family, friends — had the best of intentions to enlighten the next generation, but they actually revealed quite a bit about the messengers themselves, and a lot about the state of the world from which they grew up in. While the advice may vary, there’s no denying that a common theme prevails — their messages are shaped by what they witnessed — their economic, social and cultural experiences. But what’s most interesting to me, working within the staffing industry, is how the experiences of each generation have also shaped the way they work.

Unlike any other period in time, we have the greatest number of generations — the silent generation (born 1928-45), baby boomers (1946-64), generation X (1965-80), and generation Y/Millineals (1981-99) — within the workplace and will soon add another (2000-)! The newest, yet to be officially named (but often referred to as ReGens) is currently in school, and like all those that have come before, will surely inject its own unique needs into the workplace. April 2013
It’s time to change the music. May I have this dance?

Over the past few months we’ve all heard or read numerous accounts on how there is a talent war looming in which the search/demand for talent becomes critical. While the problem is readily apparent, the solution is not/has not been forthcoming.

Given the economic conditions over the past few years, ‘employment numbers’ have occupied the spotlight. How these numbers trend has historically been a cornerstone for forecasting an economic recovery. However, the latest plateau has caused some to pause — companies and workers alike. Everyone is increasingly cautious as they wait to see the potential impacts from the sequestration and the $85 billion government spending cuts that took effect on March 1. In addition to the seemingly incurable budget debates on Capitol Hill, their wariness is compounded by continued fuel price and payroll tax concerns.
